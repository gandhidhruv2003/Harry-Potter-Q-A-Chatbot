. . . He replaced the top of the ink bottle; pulled an old pillowcase
from under his bed; put the flashlight, A History of Magic, his essay,
quill, and ink inside it; got out of bed; and hid the lot under a
loose floorboard under his bed. Then he stood up, stretched, and
checked the time on the luminous alarm clock on his bedside table. It
was one o’clock in the morning. Harry’s stomach gave a funny jolt. He
had been thirteen years old, without realizing it, for a whole hour.
Yet another unusual thing about Harry was how little he looked forward
to his birthdays. He had never received a birthday card in his life.
The Dursleys had completely ignored his last two birthdays, and he had
no reason to suppose they would remember this one. Harry walked across
the dark room, past Hedwig’s large, empty cage, to the open window. He
